This process ensures a thorough, systematic approach to analyzing categorical data, leading to reliable conclusions and actionable insights.

In each step there will be an example for, if you're analyzing customer service channel preferences:

### Step 1: Define the Research Question and Hypotheses

Begin by clearly stating what you want to learn about your categorical data. Your research question should address whether observed frequencies match expected patterns or theoretical distributions. From this question, formulate your null hypothesis (stating no significant difference exists) and alternative hypothesis (stating a significant difference exists). This foundational step guides all subsequent analysis decisions.

* Step 1 might ask: "Do our customers use different service channels in the proportions we expect?"

### Step 2: Establish Expected Frequencies

Determine what pattern or distribution you expect your data to follow. These expectations might come from historical data, theoretical models, or business assumptions. Convert these expectations into specific numerical predictions, ensuring they account for your total sample size. This step creates the baseline against which you'll compare your actual observations.

* Step 2 would establish expected usage based on industry standards or historical data

### Step 3: Collect and Organize Observed Data

Gather your observed frequencies, ensuring your data collection method is sound and your categories are mutually exclusive and exhaustive. At this stage, verify that your sample size is adequate (typically n > 30) and that expected frequencies in each category are sufficient (usually at least 5). This preparation ensures your subsequent statistical analysis will be valid and reliable.

* Step 3 would collect actual usage data across all channels

### Step 4: Verify Test Assumptions

Before proceeding with calculations, confirm that your data meets the key assumptions of the chi-squared test. Check that observations are independent, categories are mutually exclusive, and your sample size and expected frequencies meet minimum requirements. This validation step prevents drawing incorrect conclusions from inappropriately applied statistics.

* Step 4 would verify you have enough data points in each channel

### Step 5: Perform the Statistical Analysis

Calculate the chi-squared test statistic by comparing observed to expected frequencies. Determine the degrees of freedom and select your significance level (typically α = 0.05). Use these values to find your critical value or p-value. This computational step provides the evidence needed for your decision-making.

* Step 5 would calculate whether differences are statistically significant

### Step 6: Interpret Results and Draw Conclusions

Evaluate your test results in the context of your original research question. Compare your p-value to your chosen significance level, or compare your test statistic to the critical value. Based on these comparisons, make a decision about your null hypothesis. This interpretation step bridges the gap between statistical findings and practical meaning.

* Step 6 would determine if channel usage truly differs from expectations

### Step 7: Communicate Findings and Implications

Present your results in a way that addresses your original research question and provides actionable insights. Include both statistical evidence and practical interpretations. Discuss any limitations of your analysis and suggest potential next steps. This final step transforms your statistical analysis into valuable business intelligence.

* Step 7 would recommend whether to reallocate resources across channels
