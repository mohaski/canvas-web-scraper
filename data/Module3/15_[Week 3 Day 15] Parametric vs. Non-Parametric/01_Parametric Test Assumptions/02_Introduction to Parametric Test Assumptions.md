As a data analyst, you'll frequently encounter situations where you need to compare groups, assess relationships, or evaluate the impact of variables on outcomes. Parametric tests like t-tests, ANOVA, and linear regression are powerful tools for these tasks – but their reliability depends entirely on whether your data meets certain fundamental assumptions. Think of these assumptions as the foundation of a building: if they're not solid, anything built on top becomes unstable.

In your day-to-day work, you might be asked questions like "Is our new website design significantly better than the old one?" or "Does our updated manufacturing process reduce defect rates?" While it's tempting to jump straight into running these comparisons, understanding parametric assumptions helps you avoid a common pitfall that many junior analysts face: applying tests that don't actually suit their data, potentially leading to incorrect conclusions that could cost their organization time and money.

Consider Sarah, a product analyst at an e-commerce company. She needs to analyze whether a new recommendation algorithm increases average order value. Her initial instinct is to run a t-test comparing orders before and after the algorithm change. However, order values often follow a right-skewed distribution (many small orders and fewer large ones), which challenges one of the key parametric assumptions. Understanding these assumptions helped Sarah realize she needed either to transform her data or use non-parametric alternatives to make valid conclusions.

The four main assumptions we'll explore are:

* **Normality:** The data should follow a normal distribution  
  Independence: Observations should be independent of each other
* **Homoscedasticity:** Groups should have similar variances
* **Level of Measurement:** Data should be measured at the interval or ratio leve

Throughout this topic, we'll examine each assumption in detail, learn how to test for them using both visual and statistical methods, and understand what to do when they're violated. You'll develop practical skills in using statistical software to assess these assumptions and make informed decisions about your analytical approach.

Most importantly, you'll learn not just how to check these assumptions, but how to communicate their importance to stakeholders who might not have statistical backgrounds. This skill becomes particularly valuable when you need to explain why you've chosen a specific analytical approach or when you need to justify additional data collection to meet these assumptions properly.
