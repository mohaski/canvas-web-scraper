Imagine you're a data analyst at a tech company, and your product manager comes to you with user satisfaction ratings for a new feature. The ratings are on a scale of 1-5 stars, and they want to know if users prefer the new version over the old one. You quickly realize that standard t-tests won't be appropriate here - the data is ordinal, not continuous, and definitely not normally distributed. This is where non-parametric and exact tests become invaluable tools in your analytical toolkit.

Non-parametric tests are statistical methods that don't rely on the rigid assumptions we need for parametric tests. Think of them like all-terrain vehicles - they might not be as fast as sports cars on a perfect highway (parametric tests), but they can handle rough terrain (messy, real-world data) much better. Exact tests, meanwhile, are like precision instruments that give you exact probability calculations rather than approximations, particularly useful when you're working with small samples.

When assumptions are violated, non-parametric and exact tests become our go-to solutions. They allow us to maintain statistical rigor while handling the kinds of data we commonly encounter in business settings.

* Analyzing customer satisfaction scores
* Comparing product rankings
* Evaluating survey responses
* Analyzing time-to-completion data (often right-skewed)
* Examining conversion rates across different user segments
