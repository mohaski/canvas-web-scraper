### Terms Recap

**Probability distributions** are mathematical functions that describe how likely different outcomes are to occur in a dataset. The key terms we covered include:

The **Probability Density Function (PDF)** tells us the relative likelihood of different values occurring in continuous data, like measuring heights or times.

The **Probability Mass Function (PMF)** serves a similar purpose for discrete data, like counting events.

The **Cumulative Distribution Function (CDF)** tells us the probability of a value falling below a certain point

The **Quantile Function (inverse CDF)** helps us find values corresponding to specific probabilities.

### Key Concepts

Understanding probability distributions helps us make sense of data in several important ways:

* **Natural patterns in data** tend to follow specific distributions. For example, measurement errors often follow a normal distribution, while waiting times typically follow an exponential distribution. By recognizing these patterns, we can better understand what our data is telling us.
* Different distributions serve different purposes. The **normal distribution** helps us understand symmetric data like test scores, while the **lognormal distribution** better describes skewed data like response times. Each distribution has properties that make it suitable for specific types of data.

Distributions help us move beyond simple averages to understand the full range of likely values. This deeper understanding helps us make better predictions and set more realistic expectations.

### Brief Process Overview

Working with probability distributions follows these essential steps:

1. **Understand Your Data's Nature:** Examine what you're measuring and what patterns you might expect to see based on the real-world context.
2. **Explore Basic Patterns:** Look at fundamental characteristics like central tendency and spread to get initial clues about appropriate distributions.
3. **Choose and Apply Distributions:** Select and fit appropriate distributions based on your data's characteristics and use them to answer practical questions about your data.

For example, when analyzing customer service data, this process helps us understand typical response times, set reasonable service level agreements, and identify unusual cases that need attention.
