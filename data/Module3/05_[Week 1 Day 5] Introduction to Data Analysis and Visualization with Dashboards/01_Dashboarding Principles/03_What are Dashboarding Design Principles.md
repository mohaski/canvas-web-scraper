Dashboarding is the art and science of distilling complex information into visual narratives. It's about transforming raw data into actionable insights through strategic design and presentation. At its core, dashboarding is a communication tool that bridges the gap between data complexity and human understanding.

Conceptually, dashboarding is about creating a bridge between data and decision-making, transforming numbers into knowledge, and ultimately, driving action based on evidence. It's a multidisciplinary practice that combines elements of data analysis, psychology, design, and business strategy to create powerful tools for understanding and managing complex systems and processes.

### Key Aspects of Dashboarding

#### Dashboarding

The practice of designing and implementing visual displays (dashboards) that present critical data and performance indicators in a single view, enabling quick understanding and decision-making.

#### F-Pattern

A common reading pattern where users tend to scan content in an F-shaped pattern, focusing on the top and left side of the screen.

#### Color Theory

The study of how colors interact and how they can be used effectively in visual design, including concepts like color harmony, contrast, and psychological effects of colors.

#### KPI (Key Performance Indicator)

A measurable value that demonstrates how effectively a company is achieving key business objectives.

#### Interactivity

Features that allow users to manipulate or explore data within the dashboard, such as filters or drill-down capabilities.

#### Responsiveness

The ability of a dashboard to adapt to different screen sizes and devices.

Other key aspects of dashboarding include:

1. **Information Synthesis:** Dashboarding involves curating and combining diverse data points into a coherent story. It's about identifying the most crucial information and presenting it in a way that reveals patterns, trends, and relationships.
2. **Visual Storytelling:** A dashboard is essentially a data-driven story. It guides the viewer through a narrative, highlighting key points and allowing for exploration of details.
3. **Cognitive Load Management:** Effective dashboarding reduces cognitive load by presenting information in a way that aligns with human perception and cognitive processes. This involves careful consideration of layout, color, and visual hierarchy.
4. **Decision Support:** Dashboards serve as decision-support tools, providing the right information at the right time to facilitate informed decision-making.
5. **Democratization of Data:** By making complex data accessible and understandable, dashboarding democratizes information within an organization, enabling data-driven culture at all levels.
6. **Dynamic Interaction:** Modern dashboarding often incorporates interactivity, allowing users to engage with the data, ask questions, and derive personalized insights.
7. **Contextual Framing:** Dashboards provide context to data, framing metrics and KPIs within the broader business or operational landscape.
8. **Attention Direction:** Through design principles, dashboarding directs attention to the most critical information, ensuring that key insights are not overlooked.
9. **Real-Time Monitoring:** In many cases, dashboards serve as real-time monitoring tools, providing a pulse on current performance or conditions.
10. **Insight Acceleration:** By consolidating and visualizing data, dashboarding accelerates the process of gaining insights, reducing the time from data collection to action.

### How Do Dashboarding Design Principles Work?

#### Dashboard Layout and F-Pattern Design

Dashboard layout refers to the strategic arrangement of visual elements on a dashboard to optimize information consumption and user experience. The F-pattern is a layout design that follows the natural eye movement of users in Western cultures when scanning web content.

##### F-Pattern

* Users typically scan a page in an F-shaped pattern: across the top, down the left side, and across the middle.
* This pattern influences where to place the most important information for immediate visibility.

##### Information Hierarchy

* Most critical information (e.g., KPIs) should be placed at the top of the dashboard.
* Secondary information follows in descending order of importance.

##### Visual Flow

* Guide the user's eye through the dashboard in a logical sequence.
* Use size, color, and whitespace to create a clear visual hierarchy.

##### Grouping Related Information

* Cluster related metrics or visualizations together for easier comprehension.
* Use borders, backgrounds, or spacing to delineate different sections.

##### Responsive Design

* Ensure the layout adapts well to different screen sizes and devices.
* Consider how the F-pattern might change on mobile devices (potentially becoming more linear).

##### Whitespace Utilization

* Use whitespace effectively to prevent clutter and improve readability.
* Balance between information density and visual clarity.

By implementing this F-pattern layout, you create a dashboard that naturally guides users through the most important information, enhancing their ability to quickly grasp key insights and make data-driven decisions. This approach demonstrates your understanding of user behavior and your ability to design effective, user-friendly data visualizations - valuable skills for your growing career in data science and analytics.

#### Color Theory in Dashboard Design

Color theory is the study of how colors interact and the impact they have on user perception and behavior. In dashboard design, it's used to enhance readability, guide attention, and convey meaning.

##### Color Harmony

* Use complementary, analogous, or monochromatic color schemes for visual appeal.
* Ensure colors work well together and don't clash or vibrate visually.

##### Brand Consistency

* Incorporate company brand colors to maintain visual identity.
* Use brand colors as a base and expand the palette as needed.

##### Color Psychology

* Leverage the emotional and psychological associations of colors.
* Example: Blue for trust and stability, green for growth or finance.

##### Contrast and Readability

* Ensure sufficient contrast between text and background for legibility.
* Use the [Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG)


  Links to an external site.](https://www.w3.org/WAI/standards-guidelines/wcag/) for contrast ratios.

##### Color for Categorization

* Use distinct colors to differentiate categories in charts.
* Ensure colors are distinguishable for color-blind users.

##### Color Intensity for Emphasis

* Use more saturated colors to draw attention to key metrics.
* Use softer, less saturated colors for supporting information.

By thoughtfully applying color theory, you create a dashboard that not only looks professional and aligns with brand identity but also enhances data comprehension and guides users to the most critical information. This demonstrates your ability to go beyond basic data presentation to create truly effective and user-friendly visualizations.

#### Data Visualization Best Practices

Data visualization best practices are guidelines for effectively presenting data in visual formats to facilitate understanding, insight generation, and decision-making.

##### Choose the Right Chart Type

Match the chart type to the nature of your data and the story you want to tell.

Examples:

* Line charts for time-series data and trends
* Bar charts for comparing categories
* Scatter plots for showing relationships between variables
* Heatmaps for displaying patterns in complex datasets

##### Simplify and Declutter

* Remove unnecessary elements (chart junk) that don't contribute to understanding.
* Use the data-ink ratio principle: maximize the ratio of data to non-data ink.

##### Use Color Effectively

* Use color to highlight important information or differentiate categories.
* Ensure color choices are accessible (consider color-blind users).
* Limit color palette to avoid overwhelming the viewer.

##### Provide Context

* Include relevant comparisons or benchmarks.
* Add annotations to explain significant data points or trends.

##### Label Clearly and Concisely

* Use clear, readable fonts.
* Label axes and data points directly when possible.
* Avoid angled or vertical text for readability.

##### Scale Appropriately

* Use consistent scales for similar data.
* Consider whether to start axes at zero (usually necessary for bar charts).
* Clearly indicate when scales are broken or nonlinear.

##### Arrange Information Logically

* Present information in a logical order (e.g., chronological, hierarchical).
* Group related information together.

##### Use Interactivity Judiciously

* Implement interactive elements like filters or drill-downs to allow deeper exploration.
* Ensure interactivity adds value and doesn't overcomplicate the visualization.

##### Design for Your Audience

* Consider the technical expertise and needs of your audience.
* Tailor the level of detail and complexity accordingly.

##### Ensure Consistency

* Maintain consistent formatting, labeling, and styling across visualizations.
* Use a coherent visual language throughout your dashboard or report.

By adhering to these best practices, you can create visualizations that are not only aesthetically pleasing but also highly functional, informative and represent the data accurately, making it easy for stakeholders to derive insights quickly.

#### Chart Selection and Implementation

Data visualization best practices involve choosing the most appropriate visual representations for different types of data to maximize understanding and insight generation.

Best Practices for Data Visualization

| Type of Data | Type of Chart | Rationale | Implementation |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Time-Series | Line chart/plots | Shows patterns, trends, changes across time | * Time on X-axis * Metric on Y-axis * Consistent time intervals |
| Categorical Comparisons | Bar charts | Shows comparison across groups. Can be stacked or grouped for more granularity | * Categories on X-axis * Metric on Y-axis * Sort high to low Color scheme to highlight top |
| Part-to-Whole | Stacked bars or Treemaps | Shows total values and the contribution of individual parts | * Contrast colors for easy differentiation * Include percentage or values labeled for each part |
| Distribution/Spread | Histograms or Box/Violin/Whisker | Shows spread and measures of central tendency. Easy to identify outliers and compare. | * Provide metric context (mean, median) * Use color schemes to differentiate groups |
| Correlation | Scatter plots | Shows clear numerical and linear relationships | * Use color or size or marker style to introduce third variable * Include trend line to emphasis strong relationship |

#### Dashboarding Tools

##### Tableau

* Wide spread use across Industries. Founded in 2003 out of a Stanford CS project, Tableau revolutionized visualization tools through the creation and use of VizQL, a visual query language for databases.
* This has allowed Tableau to implement a drag and drop user interface for creating visuals, making it incredibly easy to pick up and learn without needing any technical or programming skills.
* Tableau offers a free tier via the Tableau Public product as well as paid tiers for organizations and companies. Free tier is lacking some minor features and advanced capabilities but is a very good for a free product, especially for individuals just starting out with dashboarding.
* As one of the market leaders in dashboard and visualization tools, Tableau has created a unique and widespread community to help users and to share technical knowledge and visualization tips through things like their “Viz of the Day”.

##### Microsoft Power BI

* First and foremost it is a Microsoft product and thus has amazing integration capabilities with the larger Microsoft ecosystem. If your organization is using Microsoft systems they are also probably using Power BI.
* Has a free desktop version, similar to Tableau, with most of the major visualization features and capabilities available. The paid options lean heavily into the Microsoft ecosystem functionalities and integration that isn’t offered with the free tier. Generally organization wide licenses are wrapped into a pricing agreement for a suite of products.
* Another market leader in the visualization space leading to a plethora of material, internally from Microsoft and externally, that can help one learn and troubleshoot using Power BI. Prevalent usage in Fortune 500 companies due to Microsoft support.
* Very similar to Tableau in terms of interface and usage. Has a little bit more functionality in terms of data inspection, cleaning, and processing than Tableau does.

##### Qlik Sense + Qlik Cloud Analytics

* Offers a highly customizable and interactive interface with a client managed offering (Qlik Sense) or cloud based offering (Cloud Analytics). Client management can be highly beneficial for heavily regulated industries that want more security and in-house control.
* Comprehensive platform with tools for simple visualizations to complex dashboards and even into predictive analytics via its AutoML suite of tools. Allows a high level of integration between real time data updates and dashboards.
* Has a limited 30 day free trial with less integration capabilities but access to some good tools. Provides helpful training content and an interactive assistant for getting started learning.
* Stands out in its flexibility on interacting with visuals and dashboards allowing users the capability to heavily filter, subset and hone in on specific data points or sections to highlight. Also has a mobile app version for on the fly analytics.

##### Splunk by Cisco

* Integrate with cloud based database systems for powerful search and query capabilities. Highly scalable for large volumes and quantity of data being ingested.
* Offers a strong suite of security and IT related operations and capabilities including threat detection, analysis, and endpoint monitoring. Leads to strong functionality in analyzing “machine generated” data. Digital information that is automatically created by computer processes, applications, or other mechanisms without human intervention.
* Has several limited free trials. The cloud version has a 14 day free trial and the local ‘enterprise’ version has a 60 day free trial. As well as other free trials for individual products like SOAR (Splunk Security Orchestration, Automation and Response).
* Stands out for its usage in IT and Security related operations but is less suitable for more traditional BI and visualization than other options out there. Has a slightly steeper learning curve than other options as well.

This is by no means a comprehensive list of the available BI and dashboarding tools out there. Many cloud platforms run their own integrated analytics and visualization platforms, including Amazon AWS QuickSight, Oracle Analytics and of course Microsoft Power BI. Google purchased an existing platform, Looker, to integrate with the Google cloud ecosystem and compete as well. Looker was initially developed as a business intelligence tool to help companies retain customers and understand their behavior.

Dashboarding tools and capabilities also extend into deployment applications that assist in hosting and providing the dashboard to users. This also plays a large role in integrating machine learning models and predictive analytics into dashboards and deployments as you get deeper into Data Science and artificial intelligence. Tools such as Dash/Plotly, Flask, and Streamlit allow a programmer to write programming scripts that interface with the tool to produce web applications and display visuals, data, and ultimately allow users to utilize predictive models, without ever having to jump into web development languages (HTML, CSS, Javascript). While we won’t go into any specifics here it is important to know these tools exist and make us more efficient programmers!

### Conceptualize Dashboarding Design Principles

Dashboards for Business Data

| --- | --- | --- |
| Daily Sales Trend | Time-Series → line chart Annotate high and low days | Simple visual to show clear sales trends and changes across time. |
