You calculate a 95% confidence interval for customer satisfaction scores and get 7.2 to 7.8 on a 10-point scale. What does this mean?

95% of all customer scores fall between 7.2 and 7.8
:   Incorrect. This is a subtle but crucial distinction. The confidence interval doesn't tell us about individual scores or the percentage of scores in that range. Instead, it tells us about the reliability of our estimation method. Think of it like a fishing net – if you cast your net 100 times, a 95% confidence interval means you'd catch the true value in about 95 of those casts. When analyzing web page load times, you notice the original data is heavily skewed (many fast loads, few very slow ones). According to the Central Limit Theorem, what will happen if you take many samples of size 30 and calculate their means?

The sample means will follow the same skewed distribution as the original data
:   Incorrect. This is the magic of the Central Limit Theorem! Even when your original data is skewed, the distribution of sample means tends toward a normal distribution as long as your samples are large enough (usually n ≥ 30). It's like throwing pebbles in a pond – no matter how irregularly you throw them, the ripples tend to form regular patterns. The theorem works regardless of the original distribution's shape, which is why it's so powerful in statistics.
