When designing an experiment to test a new website feature, you notice that user behavior varies significantly by time of day. This variable would be considered a:

Independent variable
:   Incorrect. This is a confounding variable because time of day could affect both your independent variable (the new feature) and your dependent variable (user behavior). It's not an independent variable because we're not intentionally manipulating it as part of our experiment, and it's not a dependent variable because it's not an outcome we're trying to measure. Confounding variables need to be controlled for in our experimental design to ensure valid results, perhaps by ensuring equal distribution of tests across different times of day.

You run a hypothesis test comparing two groups and get p\_value = 0.03. What's the correct interpretation?

There's a 3% chance the null hypothesis is true
:   Incorrect. If there were no real difference, we'd see results this extreme 3% of the time Explanation: The p-value represents the probability of observing results as extreme as ours if the null hypothesis were true. It's not the probability of the null hypothesis being true. Statistical tests are not proving if something is ‘definitely’ different, there is always some level of uncertainty.

A product manager wants to test if a new feature increases user engagement. They decide to measure engagement for one week before and one week after launching the feature. What's the main problem with this experimental design?

The sample size is too small
:   Incorrect. This is a subtle question about experimental design in hypothesis testing. While sample size might seem like an issue (option a), and measurement duration could be debated (option c), the fundamental flaw is the lack of a control group measured during the same time period. Without a concurrent control group, we can't distinguish between changes caused by our features and changes caused by other factors (like day of week effects, seasonal trends, or other external events). This is called a "before-after" design, and it's weaker than a proper controlled experiment where we compare treatment and control groups during the same time period. The correct experimental design would involve randomly assigning some users to get the new feature while keeping others on the old version during the same week.
