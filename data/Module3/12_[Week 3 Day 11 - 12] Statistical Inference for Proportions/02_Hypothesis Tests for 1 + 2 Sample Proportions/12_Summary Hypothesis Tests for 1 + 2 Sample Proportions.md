### Terms Recap

Understanding hypothesis testing for proportions empowers us to make data-driven decisions when analyzing categorical data. This fundamental statistical tool helps us determine whether observed differences in proportions represent real effects or merely random chance.

When we work with proportions, we're dealing with several interconnected concepts that work together to help us draw reliable conclusions:

The null hypothesis (H₀) represents our default assumption - typically that no effect or difference exists. Like a presumption of innocence in law, it stands until evidence convinces us otherwise. For example, when comparing two website designs, our null hypothesis might state that both have the same conversion rate.

The alternative hypothesis (H₁) represents the claim we need evidence to support. It might state that one website design performs differently than the other. This hypothesis determines whether we use a one-sided or two-sided test.

The test statistic (z-score) measures how far our observed results deviate from what we'd expect under the null hypothesis. Think of it as quantifying the strength of our evidence. We calculate it by comparing our observed difference to the standard error - the amount of random variation we'd expect to see in our sampling.

The p-value tells us how likely we would be to see results as extreme as ours if the null hypothesis were true. A small p-value (typically below 0.05) suggests our evidence against the null hypothesis is strong enough to reject it.

### Key Concepts

Several fundamental ideas underpin hypothesis testing for proportions:

1. **Sampling Distribution**: The sampling distribution of differences in proportions follows a normal distribution when our samples are large enough. This mathematical foundation allows us to calculate precise probabilities and make reliable inferences.
2. **Standard Error**: The standard error represents the typical amount of random variation we'd expect to see between samples. It depends on both the sample size and the underlying proportion, becoming smaller with larger samples.
3. **Effect Size:** Beyond statistical significance, the effect size tells us the practical importance of a difference. A statistically significant result might not be practically meaningful if the effect size is very small.

### Brief Process Overview

The hypothesis testing process follows a logical progression:

1. Frame the Research Question Transform business questions into statistical hypotheses.
2. Plan the Data Collection Determine sample sizes and sampling methods.
3. State the Hypotheses Formally specify null and alternative hypotheses.
4. Verify Assumptions Check sample size requirements and independence.
5. Calculate and Compare Compute test statistics and p-values.
6. Draw and Communicate Conclusions Interpret results in context and make recommendations.
