### Quick Check

A product manager wants to test if a new website design improves conversion rates. They have historical data showing the current conversion rate is 12%. They want to be able to detect a 3 percentage point improvement. Which aspect of the required sample size calculation is most important to consider?

The significance level (α) of the test
:   Incorrect. This question tests understanding of statistical power in hypothesis testing. While all three factors affect sample size calculation, the power of the test (1 - β) is most crucial here because it directly relates to our ability to detect a specific improvement (3 percentage points). The power tells us the probability of detecting a true difference of this size when it exists. Without adequate power, we might fail to detect a real improvement of 3 percentage points even if it exists. The significance level (α) affects false positives but not our ability to detect real differences, and the standard deviation is determined by the proportion itself in this case. Understanding power is essential for making informed decisions about sample size in proportion testing.

### Quick Check

In a two-sample proportion test comparing email click-through rates, Group A had 45 clicks out of 200 emails (22.5%), and Group B had 65 clicks out of 250 emails (26%). When calculating the test statistic, why do we use the pooled proportion instead of the individual sample proportions?

To increase the sample size
:   Incorrect. This question tests understanding of the logic behind hypothesis testing. We use the pooled proportion because it represents our null hypothesis that there is no true difference between the groups. Under the null hypothesis, both groups should have the same underlying proportion, which we estimate by pooling the data. Using individual proportions would contradict the very assumption we're testing under the null hypothesis. This isn't about sample size (option a) or reducing standard error (option c) - it's about properly representing our null hypothesis assumption in our calculations. Understanding this helps us grasp why the test statistic is constructed the way it is.

### Quick Check

A quality control analyst tests if a manufacturing process meets a 99% quality standard. After testing 100 items and finding 98 good items (98%), they fail to reject the null hypothesis that the true proportion equals 99%. If they test 10,000 items with the same 98% success rate, they do reject the null hypothesis. Why does this happen?

The larger sample violated the test assumptions
:   Incorrect. This question tests understanding of how sample size affects hypothesis testing. With a larger sample size, the test becomes more sensitive to smaller differences from the null hypothesis. This occurs because the standard error decreases with larger sample sizes, making it easier to detect even small deviations from the hypothesized value. The test assumptions weren't violated (option a) - in fact, they're better met with larger samples. The significance level doesn't change with sample size (option b). This illustrates why we need to consider both statistical and practical significance: with very large samples, even tiny differences can be statistically significant. Understanding this relationship between sample size and sensitivity helps us interpret test results appropriately and plan studies effectively.
