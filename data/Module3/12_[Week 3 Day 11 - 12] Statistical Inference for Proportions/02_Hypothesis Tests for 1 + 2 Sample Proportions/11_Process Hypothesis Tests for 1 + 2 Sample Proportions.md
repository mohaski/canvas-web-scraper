### Step 1: Frame the Research Question

We begin by translating a business or research question into precise statistical terms. For instance, instead of asking "Is our new website better?", we specify "Does the new website design have a higher conversion rate than the current design?" This critical first step shapes everything that follows, just as a detective's initial question guides their investigation.

### Step 2: Plan the Data Collection

Before gathering any data, we need to determine how much evidence we'll need to draw reliable conclusions. This involves:

* Calculating required sample sizes based on how small a difference we want to detect
* Ensuring our sampling method will give us representative data
* Setting up proper tracking to collect the right information

Think of this as planning what evidence to gather before starting an investigation, rather than hoping you'll find the right clues by chance.

### Step 3: State the Hypotheses

Now we precisely state what we're testing. We always start with a null hypothesis (H₀) that assumes no effect or no difference exists. This is like presuming innocence in a legal case. Our alternative hypothesis (H₁) is what we need evidence to support. For example:

* H₀: The new website's conversion rate equals the old website's rate (p₁ = p₂)
* H₁: The new website's conversion rate differs from the old website's rate (p₁ ≠ p₂)

### Step 4: Verify Assumptions

Before analyzing our data, we must check if our evidence is reliable. Just as a detective verifies their evidence hasn't been contaminated, we verify our data meets statistical requirements:

* Are our samples large enough? (np ≥ 10 and n(1-p) ≥ 10)
* Are our observations independent?
* Was our sampling truly random? If these conditions aren't met, our conclusions might not be trustworthy.

### Step 5: Calculate and Compare

This is where we evaluate our evidence. We:

* Calculate our test statistic (z-score)
* Determine our p-value (probability of seeing such results if null hypothesis is true)
* Compare against our significance level (usually 0.05) This is similar to a detective weighing evidence against a standard of proof.

### Step 6: Draw and Communicate Conclusions

Finally, we interpret what our evidence tells us, considering both statistical and practical significance. We need to:

* State our conclusion in clear terms
* Explain the evidence supporting our conclusion
* Discuss practical implications
* Acknowledge any limitations

Each step builds naturally on the previous ones. If we skip steps or perform them out of order, we risk drawing unreliable conclusions. For example, if we collect data before determining required sample sizes (jumping from Step 1 to Step 3), we might not have enough evidence to draw meaningful conclusions.

Think of these steps as a funnel, starting with broad questions and systematically narrowing down to specific, supported conclusions. Each step has a specific purpose:

* Steps 1-2 ensure we're asking the right question and gathering appropriate evidence
* Steps 3-4 establish our framework for evaluation
* Step 5 conducts the actual analysis
* Step 6 translates statistical findings into actionable insights
