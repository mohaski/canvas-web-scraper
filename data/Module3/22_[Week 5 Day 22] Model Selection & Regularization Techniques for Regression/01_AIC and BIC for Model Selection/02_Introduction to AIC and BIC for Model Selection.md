In this lesson, we explore **Akaike Information Criterion (AIC)** and **Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC)** as tools for model selection in Multiple Linear Regression (MLR) models. Both AIC and BIC are statistical metrics that balance the trade-off between model complexity (number of predictors) and goodness of fit. These tools allow us to systematically compare different models to identify the one that provides the most accurate predictions without unnecessary complexity.

The Boston Housing dataset serves as the basis for this lesson, offering a real-world example of regression modeling. The dataset includes housing prices (medv) as the response variable and various predictors, such as:

* **CRIM:** Crime rate by town
* **RM:** Average number of rooms per dwelling
* **LSTAT:** Percentage of lower-status population

### Real-World Application

Imagine you are a data analyst working for a real estate company. Your task is to **develop a predictive model** to estimate housing prices based on factors such as crime rates, average rooms, and socioeconomic metrics. The company needs the most effective model to:

* **Identify key factors** driving housing prices.
* **Avoid overly complex models** that are hard to interpret or apply in production systems.

By **systematically applying AIC and BIC**, you can compare models, remove unnecessary predictors, and strike the ideal balance between simplicity and accuracy. This approach mirrors day-to-day workflows in data analysis and machine learning where model selection plays a crucial role.
