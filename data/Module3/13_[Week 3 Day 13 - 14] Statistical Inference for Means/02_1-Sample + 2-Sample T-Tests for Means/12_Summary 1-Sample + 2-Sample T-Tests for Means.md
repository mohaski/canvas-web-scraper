### Terms

T-tests serve as statistical tools that help us make informed decisions about means when working with sample data. At their core, they measure whether differences we observe are likely to be real or just due to random chance. They come in three main varieties: one-sample tests for comparing against a standard, independent two-sample tests for comparing unrelated groups, and paired tests for comparing matched measurements.

The foundation rests on several critical components:

* The t-statistic measures how many standard errors an observed difference is from zero
* The p-value tells us how likely our results would be if there were no real effect
* Confidence intervals give us a range of plausible values for the true difference
* Effect size helps us understand the practical importance of our findings

### Key Concepts

t-tests requires grasping several fundamental ideas. First, we're always working with samples to make inferences about populations. Second, we need to account for both the size of a difference and its variability. Third, larger sample sizes give us more precise estimates and greater ability to detect real differences. Finally, paired designs can be more powerful than independent samples when measuring the same subjects twice.
