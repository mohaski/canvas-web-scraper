The T-Test Decision Making Process

### 1. Defining Your Research Question

Before diving into any statistical analysis, you need to clearly articulate what you're trying to learn. This involves identifying:

* The specific metric you're investigating (like load times or customer satisfaction scores)
* What would constitute a meaningful difference in your context
* Your comparison target (whether it's a standard value or another group)
* The practical implications of finding or not finding a difference

### 2. Selecting the Appropriate T-Test

Based on your research question, determine which type of t-test best fits your needs:

* One-sample t-test if you're comparing against a known standard
* Independent t-test if you're comparing two unrelated groups
* Paired t-test if you're comparing before/after measurements or matched pairs

This choice fundamentally affects how you'll collect and analyze your data, so it needs to be made early in the process.

### 3. Planning Data Collection

With your test type selected, plan how you'll gather your data to ensure valid results:

* Determine required sample size based on desired statistical power
* Establish sampling methodology to ensure representativeness
* Define clear measurement procedures to maintain consistency
* Consider potential sources of bias and how to minimize them

This planning stage is crucial for ensuring your results will be meaningful and trustworthy.

### 4. Validating Test Assumptions

Before running your analysis, verify that your data meets the key assumptions:

* Check for approximate normality using visualizations and formal tests
* Assess independence of observations
* For paired tests, confirm proper matching of pairs

Understanding whether these assumptions are met helps you decide if you need to modify your approach.

### 5. Conducting the Analysis

Once your assumptions are verified, perform your t-test and calculate:

* The test statistic
* The p-value
* Confidence intervals for the mean(s)
* Effect size measures to understand practical significance

This gives you the statistical evidence needed for decision-making.

### 6. Interpreting and Communicating Results

Finally, translate your statistical findings into actionable insights:

* Consider both statistical and practical significance
* Frame results in terms of your original research question
* Acknowledge any limitations or uncertainties
* Make clear, justified recommendations based on your findings

Each step in this process builds upon the previous ones, creating a logical flow from question to answer. The key is understanding that t-tests aren't just statistical procedures - they're tools for making informed decisions about real business questions.

Remember: Your goal isn't just to get a p-value; it's to answer meaningful questions about your data in a way that leads to better decisions. This process helps ensure that your analysis serves that larger purpose rather than becoming a mere mathematical exercise.

By following this structured approach, you're more likely to conduct analyses that are not only statistically sound but also practically useful for your organization's decision-making needs.
