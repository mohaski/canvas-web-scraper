### Quick Check

A product manager wants to determine if customer satisfaction scores improved after a website redesign. They measure satisfaction scores for different customers before and after the change. Which type of t-test should they use?

One-sample t-test
:   Incorrect. This is a great question to test your understanding of when to use different types of t-tests. While we're comparing "before" and "after" measurements, we're measuring different customers in each group. The key here is that these are independent groups - knowing one customer's score tells us nothing about any score in the other group. We're not measuring the same customers twice, which would require a paired test. A one-sample test wouldn't work because we're comparing two groups, not one group against a standard value. This scenario is a classic case for an independent two-sample t-test.

### Quick Check

Your t-test returns a p-value of 0.03. What does this tell you about your results?

There's a 3% chance your null hypothesis is true
:   Incorrect. This question tackles one of the most commonly misunderstood aspects of statistical testing. The p-value isn't the probability that your null hypothesis is true (option a), nor does it give you a direct measure of certainty about a real difference (option c). Instead, it tells you how likely your observed results would be if there truly were no effect. Think of it like this: if you flip a fair coin 10 times and get 9 heads, that would be unlikely - but that unlikelihood doesn't prove the coin is unfair. Similarly, a small p-value tells us our results would be unlikely under the null hypothesis, but it's about probability of data, not probability of hypotheses.

### Quick Check

An analyst runs a t-test comparing processing times between two servers and finds a statistically significant difference (p < 0.05), but the actual difference in mean processing time is only 0.1 milliseconds. What's the most appropriate conclusion?

The servers are definitely performing differently and we should switch to the faster one
:   Incorrect. This question helps us understand the crucial distinction between statistical and practical significance. With a large enough sample size, we can detect very small differences that are statistically significant (meaning they're probably real differences), but that doesn't automatically make them important in practice. Think about weighing yourself on two different scales - even if one consistently shows a 0.1-gram difference, is that difference meaningful enough to matter? Statistical tests tell us about the reliability of our findings, but we still need to use judgment about whether those findings are practically important. This is why it's crucial to consider effect size and practical implications, not just p-values.
