### Terms Recap

A confidence interval for a mean represents our best estimate of a population parameter, combining a point estimate (sample mean) with a measure of uncertainty (margin of error). Think of it as a range of plausible values for the true population mean. The interval comes with a confidence level, typically 95%, telling us how reliable our estimation process is. The margin of error, calculated using the standard error and a critical value, creates the width of our interval and reflects our precision.

### Key Concepts

Our interval follows the formula:
![LaTeX: x̄ ± t \*( s/\sqrt{n})](https://learning.flatironschool.com/equation\_images/x%25CC%2584%2520%25C2%25B1%2520t%2520\*(%2520s%252F%255Csqrt%257Bn%257D)?scale=1)
, where:

* x̄ represents our sample mean
* t is our critical value from the t-distribution
* s gives our sample standard deviation
* n shows our sample size
* s/√n calculates our standard error

### Brief Process Overview

Working with confidence intervals follows a natural progression:

1. Defining Your Estimation Goal - Clearly identifying what population parameter you're trying to estimate and determining your required confidence level
2. Planning Your Sample Collection - Determining appropriate sample size and collection methods
3. Assessing Sample Quality - Validating your data meets necessary conditions
4. Computing Interval Components - Calculating sample statistics and critical values
5. Constructing and Validating the Interval - Building and checking your confidence interval
6. Interpreting and Communicating Results - Translating statistical findings into actionable insights

Practical Applications Confidence intervals serve multiple purposes in data analysis:

* Estimating population parameters with known precision
* Making data-driven decisions while accounting for uncertainty
* Communicating results clearly to stakeholders
* Setting realistic performance targets and monitoring systems

Common Pitfalls to Avoid Understanding what confidence intervals aren't is just as important as knowing what they are:

* They don't contain 95% of individual values
* They don't guarantee accuracy if sampling bias exists
