In this topic, you were introduced to the foundational concepts of a sample and a population – a population being the entire set of possible examples relevant to a given problem and a sample being a smaller subset of that population.

You saw how data collection is, in essence, a sampling process from a given population.  Appropriate sampling strategies are an essential if the dataset is to reflect the population at large and be of use in inference. To that end, you were introduced to four key sampling strategies: simple random, stratified, cluster, and sequential sampling. We saw that each of these sampling strategies had their strengths and weaknesses and were appropriate in different situations. These strategies, at a high level, were:

* **Simple random**: Every member of the population has an equal chance of being selected.
* **Stratified:** The population is divided into groups, and a random sample is chosen from each group.
* **Cluster:** The population is divided into groups, and some of these groups are randomly selected for the entire sample.
* **Sequential:** Samples are taken one at a time, and the decision to stop sampling is based on the information gathered so far.

When collecting your own data you should be thinking of an appropriate sampling strategy given your resources and the problem at hand. And even if you are in a situation where you are not directly confronted with sampling issues – particularly as you grab pre-built datasets from various repositories like Kaggle or are on the job and handed a dataset by your boss –  these issues should still lurk in the back of your mind.

If you find that your cleaning and analysis was sound and yet does not generalize well in the wild, then the real problem could be your data and how it was collected. You can then use business understanding and any documentation on data collection and definitions to see where the problem might lie and see how to resample your data accordingly.
