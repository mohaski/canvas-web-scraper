Data quality is a key factor in ensuring that conclusions of a given analysis are sufficiently general and robust to be of use in the real world. One facet of this is ensuring that your data is clean, error-free, and formed in the right way to answer a given question. We dealt with some of these considerations when learning about how to represent data types and various methods that could be used to clean your data. But there is another equally important aspect of data quality that we have not addressed until now. Does your dataset accurately reflect the set of phenomena you wish to analyze in the world at large?

This topic focuses on this central question and how the process of data collection plays a crucial role in achieving this goal. You will be introduced to **data collection** as a process of **sampling** from all the elements in the world (i.e. the **population**) relevant to your research question. The way in which the data has been sampled directly impacts how representative it is of the population – and consequently how well any analysis conducted on your data sample generalizes to the population. We will take a look at a few canonical sampling strategies:

* Simple random sampling
* Stratified sampling
* Cluster sampling
* Sequential sampling

Which one you choose will largely depend on how your population is structured and what resources you have available for data collection.
