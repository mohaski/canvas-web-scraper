Now, let’s explore two real-world examples that demonstrate how organizations use different data types to address complex problems. These examples demonstrate how structured numeric and categorical data, as well as unstructured text, are crucial in research and surveillance activities. Both of these examples highlight the importance of accurate data representation and the techniques used to achieve meaningful insights.

### Pew Research: Studying the Digital Divide

![Pew Research Center Logo](https://moringa.instructure.com/courses/1406/files/624709/download)

Logo image courtesy of [Pew Research Center


Links to an external site.](https://www.pewresearch.org/)

#### **Problem**

Pew Research frequently studies the digital divide, which refers to the gap between those who have access to technology and the internet and those who don’t. This research is used to inform policy decisions aimed at bridging this divide. Data is collected from diverse sources, including surveys on platforms, public forum analysis, and website scraping (with permission). The data collected ranges from structured numeric and categorical data in surveys to unstructured free text from public forums.

#### **Data Science Solution**

A key issue in this research is representation bias across groups. There are significant imbalances in the number of members belonging to various ethnicities, income groups, etc. To address this, Pew Research employs stratified sampling, a technique that ensures the collected data accurately reflects the proportions of various categories within the overall population. By doing so, they can generate tractable datasets that provide a balanced representation of different groups, leading to more accurate and actionable insights.

### Center for Disease Control (CDC)

![CDC worker holding a sample or specimen tray](https://moringa.instructure.com/courses/1406/files/625257/download)

#### **Problem**

The CDC uses a multi-pronged approach for disease surveillance, gathering data from electronic health records (EHR), public health surveys, and biosurveillance systems to track trends. This results in datasets comprising structured numeric data (e.g., age, cholesterol levels, metabolic data) and categorical data (e.g., diagnosis and medication codes), as well as unstructured free text from doctor notes and public health surveys.

#### **Data Science Solution**

The CDC employs various sampling strategies depending on the situation and the data they need to assess. For example, during epidemic outbreaks, the CDC might use cluster sampling to gain a comprehensive picture of the affected population. In this method, the CDC selects a representative collection of rural hospitals (i.e., clusters) throughout the country and samples all patient cases within these hospitals. This approach provides a robust dataset that is representative of the outbreak across rural America, allowing the CDC to effectively monitor and respond to the epidemic.
