# Introduction to Measures of Dispersion

Measures of central tendency tell us where values of a given feature are clustered around. It yields a single characteristic value as a summary of the data. But this tells us nothing about the scale of variation about that characteristic value. Understanding the range of values that a variable generally takes on is often as important as knowing the most probable or central value. 

As an example, imagine an online store that tracks the average time it takes customers to receive their orders (central tendency). Without understanding the range of delivery times (dispersion), it's difficult to assess the end-to-end quality of the fulfillment process. A high dispersion could highlight potential issues—with some deliveries taking much longer than others. The converse is that a low range suggests a more consistent and reliable delivery process. The online store thus would want to characterize dispersion and understand factors influencing variability in delivery times.

We will cover a few common ways of characterizing dispersion: variance and standard deviation, the mean absolute deviation, and the interquartile range (IQR). These measures of dispersion complement measures of central tendency (the mean, median, and mode) to yield a more complete picture of a set of data and its distribution.
