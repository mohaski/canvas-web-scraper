# Activity 1: Audience-Calibrated Data Story

## **Background & Framing**

One of the most common data communication failures is audience mismatch: the analyst presents the data the way they understand it, not the way the audience needs to receive it. This activity trains you to deliberately calibrate your message.

You will work with a single dataset and craft two different narratives, one for a technical colleague, one for a non-technical executive. The goal is not to simplify or dumb down the data. It is to find the right level of abstraction, the right visual language, and the right action orientation for each audience.

## **The Dataset**

|  |
| --- |
| **Dataset: Kenya Mobile Money Adoption (2018–2023)**  You are a data analyst at a Nairobi-based fintech startup. Your team has been analyzing mobile money adoption across five counties in Kenya. Here is a summary of the key findings from your EDA:     • Nairobi County: adoption grew from 67% (2018) to 89% (2023)  • Mombasa County: adoption grew from 54% to 78%  • Kisumu County: adoption grew from 41% to 71% — highest relative growth  • Nakuru County: adoption grew from 38% to 65%  • Garissa County: adoption grew from 22% to 44% — still significantly below national average     Additional findings from statistical analysis:  • Strong correlation (r = 0.73) between smartphone penetration and mobile money adoption  • Users aged 18–34 account for 61% of new registrations  • Merchant acceptance (number of registered M-PESA merchants) is the single strongest predictor of sustained adoption (beta = 0.81, p < 0.001)  • Garissa's low adoption is partly explained by lower merchant acceptance (only 2.1 merchants per 1,000 residents vs national average of 8.4) |

 

## **Your Task**

Write TWO versions of a data narrative based on the dataset above.

### **Version A — For a Technical Colleague (Data Scientist / Analyst)**

|  |
| --- |
| **Instructions**  Your colleague is reviewing your analysis before it goes to leadership. They are comfortable with statistical language, correlation coefficients, regression terminology, and data quality caveats.     Write 150–200 words. Include:  • The key statistical findings with appropriate precision (do not round aggressively)  • One methodological note or caveat the colleague should be aware of  • A recommendation for what additional analysis should be done before presenting to leadership     You may reference chart types (e.g., scatter plot, time series) but do not need to produce actual visualizations for this activity. |

### **Version B — For a Non-Technical Executive**

|  |
| --- |
| **Instructions**  The CEO of your startup is presenting to potential investors next week. She needs to understand the headline story and what action to take, she does not need the statistical details.     Write 100–130 words. Include:  • The single most important insight in plain language  • One concrete business opportunity the data suggests  • The key risk or caveat, framed in business terms (not statistical terms)     Avoid all statistical jargon (p-values, beta coefficients, r values). If you reference numbers, round them and explain what they mean in plain English. |

 

### **Version C — Self-Reflection (Required)**

|  |
| --- |
| **Instructions**  After writing both versions, respond to these three questions in 100–150 words total:     1. What specific language or framing choices did you change between Version A and B? List at least three concrete differences.  2. What was the hardest part of writing Version B? Why?  3. How might the advice Hans Rosling demonstrated in the TED talk apply to how you would present this data visually (if you had to build a chart)? |

 

## **Activity 1 Rubric**

|  |  |  |  |  |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Criterion** | **Excellent (4)** | **Proficient (3)** | **Developing (2)** | **Beginning (1)** |
| **Audience Calibration (Version A vs B)** | Clear, consistent distinction in language, statistical depth, and framing between both versions | Noticeable difference between versions; minor instances of jargon crossing over | Some differences but versions feel similar in tone or vocabulary | Versions are nearly identical or audience is ignored |
| **Technical Accuracy** | All statistics cited correctly; methodological caveat is meaningful and specific | Statistics mostly accurate; caveat is present but could be more specific | One or two numerical errors; caveat is generic | Multiple errors or key statistics are absent |
| **Executive Clarity (Version B)** | Insight is immediately clear; opportunity and risk are framed in business language; zero jargon | Mostly clear; one jargon term slips through; opportunity is identified | Somewhat clear but buried in qualifications or partial jargon | Unclear or still highly technical |
| **Reflection Quality** | Identifies 3+ specific changes with genuine insight; Rosling connection is thoughtful and original | Identifies 3 changes; Rosling connection is present but surface level | Fewer than 3 changes identified; reflection is brief | Reflection is missing or one sentence |
