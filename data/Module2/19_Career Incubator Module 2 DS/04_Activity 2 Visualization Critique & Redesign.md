# Activity 2: Visualization Critique & Redesign

## **What To Expect**

Most bad visualizations are not made by careless people, they are made by people who are too close to their own data to see what the chart actually communicates to someone seeing it for the first time. This activity trains you to look at visualizations from the outside in.

 

## **Part 1 — Critique a Real-World Visualization (20 minutes)**

|  |
| --- |
| **Step 1: Choose a visualization to critique**     Find a data visualization published in the last 6 months from any of these sources: The Economist, Reuters Graphics, Our World in Data, or a Kenyan news source such as The Standard or Nation Media Group. Paste the URL or screenshot into your submission. |

 

|  |
| --- |
| **Step 2: Apply the CLEAR Framework**  Evaluate the visualization against each dimension below. For each, write 2–4 sentences of critique:     C — Clarity: Can a first-time viewer understand the main message in under 10 seconds? What is clear, what is not?  L — Labeling: Are axes, legends, and data points labeled with enough context? Is anything ambiguous?  E — Encoding: Is the visual encoding (color, size, position, length) appropriate for the data type? Is anything misleading?  A — Audience: Who is the likely intended audience? Is the complexity level appropriate for them?  R — Recommendation: What is the single most important change that would improve this visualization? |

 

## **Part 2 — Redesign Sketch (25 minutes)**

|  |
| --- |
| **Instructions**  Based on your critique, sketch a redesign of the visualization that addresses the most critical problems you identified.     Your redesign can be:  • Hand-drawn on paper (photograph and upload), perfectly acceptable and often faster  • Created in any digital tool: Excel, Google Sheets, Flourish, Datawrapper, Matplotlib, ggplot, or any other  • A wireframe sketch (does not need to be polished) with annotations     Required annotations on your redesign sketch:  • Label each design change you made with a brief note explaining WHY (e.g., 'Changed to bar chart, bar length is easier to compare than pie slice area')  • Indicate the intended audience at the top of the sketch  • Indicate the single main message the chart should communicate     You do not need to reproduce the actual dataset, the redesign should illustrate your design thinking, not produce a publication-ready chart. |

**Activity 2 Rubric**

|  |  |  |  |  |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Criterion** | **Excellent (4)** | **Proficient (3)** | **Developing (2)** | **Beginning (1)** |
| **Critique Depth (CLEAR Framework)** | All 5 CLEAR dimensions addressed with specific, evidence-based observations; demonstrates strong visual literacy | 4 of 5 dimensions addressed thoughtfully; observations are specific | 3 dimensions addressed; some observations are vague | 2 or fewer dimensions; critique is surface-level |
| **Redesign — Design Rationale** | Each design choice is annotated with clear, data-visualization-principled reasoning | Most changes are annotated; reasoning is present but some choices unexplained | Annotations are present but brief or not connected to critique | Few or no annotations; changes are unexplained |
| **Redesign — Clarity Improvement** | Redesign demonstrably improves the main message clarity; audience is clearly addressed | Redesign is an improvement; one or two issues remain | Some improvement but introduces new clarity problems | Redesign does not clearly improve on the original |
