Databases are essential tools for organizing, storing, and retrieving data. Imagine a library filled with books. Without a system to categorize and locate these books, finding a specific one would be a daunting task. Databases solve a similar problem for digital information. They allow us to store vast amounts of data—from customer details at a bank to your friend list on social media—and easily access what we need, when we need it.

Databases come in different types, with the most common being relational databases. These organize data into tables, which are like spreadsheets, where information is stored in rows and columns. Each table relates to another in a meaningful way, which simplifies data management and retrieval.

To interact with these databases, we use SQL , or Structured Query Language. (The initialism is usually pronounced “SE-quel” or “ess-kew-ell.”) SQL allows us to ask complex questions of our data, like retrieving all customer transactions over a certain amount or finding a friend who lives near us. It’s a powerful tool that works across different types of databases, making it indispensable for businesses and individuals to extract meaningful information from vast pools of data. Understanding databases and SQL helps us appreciate how digital services manage and use the data we interact with every day.

### Examples of Databases

* Every organization of any size uses some kind of structure to hold their data. In the smallest companies this could be a spreadsheet or multiple spreadsheets in a workbook. As the organization grows, so does its data and databases exist to hold all of it.
* SQL is used by just about every major data-driven organization out there, including Facebook, Microsoft, LinkedIn, and Accenture
* In fact, SQL was ranked as the most used database environment and the third most popular programming language by StackOverflow in 2021.

### Industry Example

Imagine you're a data scientist analyzing response times at a call center. The mean response time —the average time to answer a call—seems like a straightforward choice. However, call centers often experience outliers—complex calls that take much longer. The mean can be inflated by these outliers, misrepresenting the typical wait time for most customers.

For instance, a high mean response time might exist due to a few exceptionally long calls, while most calls are handled within a reasonable timeframe. This skewed distribution calls for a different measure of central tendency.

The median, the "middle" response time when calls are arranged from shortest to longest, is a more robust option in this scenario. Unlike the mean, the median is less influenced by outliers, providing a clearer picture of the typical customer experience. By understanding data distribution and using the median, you can make data-driven decisions to optimize call center operations and improve customer satisfaction. This can include setting realistic performance targets for call representatives, identifying bottlenecks that might be causing longer wait times for specific issues, and ultimately ensuring a smoother experience for the majority of customers.
